In 1895, Watson joined the National Cash Register Company (NCR) as a salesman. His outstanding sales performance quickly earned him a promotion to the general sale manger. At NCR, he gained valuable experience in aggressive sales techniques, business management, and customer relations-skills that would later be instrumental in his transformation of IBM. However, his time at NCR ended in controversy. in 1912, Watson was convicted in the antitrust case relates to NCR’s monopolistic practices. Despite this setback, it ultimately led to his significant role at CTR, which would later become IBM. Watson developed a reputation for his assertive and motivational style, encouraging his team to set high standards for themselves. At NCR, he introduced rigorous training programs, motivating his sales staff to be well-prepared and customer-focused. However, Watson was also involved in a controversial anti-competition campaign at NCR. This experience ended with a legal challenge, after which Watson left NCR. he time at NCR helped Watson hone his skills in sales, team management, and corporate culture-building. He learned the importance of establishing a strong brand identity and managing customer relationships—principles that he carried to IBM.

Founding Principles and Early Influence at IBM

After joining IBM (then called CTR) in 1914, Watson began to introduce the core values and management strategies he had developed at NCR. His emphasis on customer service, employee development, and innovation quickly became central to IBM’s culture. Watson introduced the “THINK” slogan, which encouraged employees to approach their work with thoughtfulness and purpose. This motto became iconic, symbolizing IBM’s commitment to intelligence and strategy. Drawing from his NCR experience, Watson established training programs at IBM that emphasized quality, discipline, and customer care. He encouraged employees to maintain high standards and continuously improve. Watson's understanding of brand identity led him to position IBM as a company known for integrity, innovation, and reliability. His focus on a strong, unified corporate culture became one of IBM’s key strengths. His foundational work at IBM set the stage for its transformation into a global leader. His commitment to innovation, quality, and customer satisfaction became guiding principles that still influence IBM today.

Legacy

Thomas J. Watson Sr. retired from IBM in 1956, the year of his death, after transforming the company into a leader in business machines and computing. During his tenure, IBM’s revenues grew more than tenfold. His legacy was continued by his son, Thomas J. Watson Jr., who led IBM into the computer age, making it one of the world's most influential technology companies. Beyond business, Watson Sr. was also a philanthropist and advocate for education, contributing to causes that reflected his commitment to innovation and social responsibility.
